The Islamic calendar, also known as the Hijri calendar, is a lunar calendar consisting of 12 months in a year of 354 or 355 days. It is used to determine the proper days of Islamic holidays and rituals, such as Ramadan, Hajj, and Eid.

The calendar began in 622 CE, marking the Hijrah (migration) of Prophet Muhammad ï·º from Makkah to Madinah. Each month begins with the sighting of the new crescent moon.

Did you know? The Islamic year is approximately 11 days shorter than the Gregorian (solar) year, which means Islamic months shift throughout the seasons over time.

ğŸ“– The Four Sacred Months

Allah says in the Qur'an: "Indeed, the number of months with Allah is twelve [lunar] months in the register of Allah [from] the day He created the heavens and the earth; of these, four are sacred." (Qur'an 9:36)

The four sacred months are: Muharram, Rajab, Dhul-Qi'dah, and Dhul-Hijjah. During these months, fighting was traditionally forbidden, and sins are considered more grave while good deeds are more rewarded.
